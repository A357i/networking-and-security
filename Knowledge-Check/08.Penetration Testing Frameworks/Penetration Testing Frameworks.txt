1. CONCEPT SUMMARY
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Penetration testing frameworks are structured methodologies that guide a tester through an engagement from scoping to reporting. They ensure thoroughness, consistency, compliance, and clear communication. No single framework fits all engagements — selection is driven by scope, regulation, and client environment. MITRE ATT&CK is not a framework but a knowledge base used to annotate findings with standardized adversary technique vocabulary.

You finish a CTF box. You gained initial access via a phishing simulation, escalated privileges by exploiting a misconfigured service, and exfiltrated a flag from a database. 5. TEACHING PROMPTS
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain penetration testing frameworks to:

A) A complete beginner (no jargon):
"Imagine you're a home inspector. You could just wander around and check whatever catches your eye — but you might miss the roof, or check the kitchen twice. A framework is a checklist that ensures you inspect every room in the right order, so nothing gets missed and your report means something."

B) A cybersecurity student:
"A pentest framework is a structured methodology that defines how to scope, conduct, and report a penetration test. Different frameworks suit different contexts — PTES for standard network tests, OWASP WSTG for web apps, NIST SP 800-115 for government work. MITRE ATT&CK isn't a framework — it's a vocabulary for describing what you found in terms of real adversary behavior."

C) A technical interviewer:
"I select frameworks based on scope, regulatory requirements, and deliverable expectations. For a standard corporate network test, PTES gives me the end-to-end workflow. I supplement it with OWASP WSTG for any web components and annotate findings with MITRE ATT&CK technique IDs to give the client's blue team actionable detection guidance. If the client is in a regulated sector — PCI DSS, UK finance — the regulatory framework takes precedence and shapes the scope and reporting format."

D) A junior pentester you're mentoring:
"You don't pick a framework arbitrarily. Ask four questions: What am I testing? What does the client's regulation require? Do they need repeatable metrics? What can my team actually implement? The answers tell you which framework to use. PTES is a solid default to learn first — it mirrors how real engagements actually run."

7. COMMON MISTAKES
─────────────────────────────────────────────
- Treating MITRE ATT&CK as a pentest framework — it is a knowledge base, not a methodology
- Selecting a framework by personal preference instead of client context and regulatory requirements
- Assuming PTES is always the right answer — it is a strong default, but regulation can override preference entirely
- Using ISSAF's tool-specific guidance — it references decade-old software versions. Study the nine-step logic, not the tools.
- Checklist mentality with WSTG — executing all 90+ test cases without assessing overall risk posture produces technically compliant but practically weak results
- Skipping the PTES pre-engagement phase — unclear scope and missing authorization letters are the most common source of legal disputes in penetration testing
- Confusing tactics and techniques in ATT&CK — tactics are the *why* (high-level objective), techniques are the *how* (specific method)
