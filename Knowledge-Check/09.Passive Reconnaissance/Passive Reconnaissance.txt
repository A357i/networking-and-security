1. CONCEPT SUMMARY
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Passive reconnaissance gathers intelligence from publicly available sources without sending any packets to the target. It is the stealthiest and lowest-risk phase of an engagement. Core techniques include WHOIS/RDAP lookups, DNS record queries, subdomain discovery via DNSDumpster and Certificate Transparency logs, and Shodan searches for exposed services. Even though passive recon is undetectable by the target, it still requires valid engagement authorisation.

5. TEACHING PROMPTS
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Explain passive reconnaissance to:

A) A complete beginner:
"Imagine you are about to buy a house. Before visiting, you look it up on Google Maps, check the property history online, and read public records. You haven't knocked on the door yet — you haven't even driven past. Passive recon is exactly that. You are gathering everything publicly available about a target without them ever knowing you looked."

B) A cybersecurity student:
"Passive recon uses publicly available sources — DNS records, WHOIS databases, certificate logs, and Shodan — to build a picture of the target without touching it. The key constraint is zero direct interaction with the target. Queries go to public resolvers and third-party databases, not the target's own infrastructure."

C) A technical interviewer:
"My passive recon workflow starts with WHOIS/RDAP for registrar and date information, then dig for A, MX, and TXT records using a public resolver like 1.1.1.1. For subdomain discovery I combine DNSDumpster and crt.sh CT log searches — crt.sh consistently returns significantly more coverage. I then feed discovered IPs into Shodan to identify exposed services and version banners. All of this happens before a single packet touches the target."

D) A junior pentester you're mentoring:
"Do not skip passive recon to get to scanning faster. The subdomains you find on crt.sh before you touch anything often include development servers, forgotten admin panels, and staging environments that are far easier to exploit than the main target. Map everything passively first."

7. COMMON MISTAKES
─────────────────────────────────────────────
- Relying on dig/nslookup alone for subdomain discovery — these only resolve names you already know; CT logs reveal far more
- Treating passive recon as legally risk-free — it still requires authorisation; "passive" describes the technique, not the legal status
- Ignoring TXT records — SPF misconfigurations, DMARC gaps, and CTF flags are commonly found here
- Using nslookup as the primary tool in professional work — dig is the standard; nslookup is legacy
- Searching Shodan by domain name only — feeding specific IPs from your dig output produces richer, more targeted results
- Skipping RDAP/WHOIS because "personal details are redacted" — dates and name servers remain valuable even without contact info
- Forgetting that crt.sh uses `%` as a wildcard, not `*` — `%.target.com` is the correct search pattern
